Streaming Device Buyer's Guide: What Actually Matters

Updated April 2026 · By the BestFirestick team

Streaming device specs are designed to confuse you. Manufacturers throw around terms like HDR10+, Dolby Atmos, WiFi 6E, and "AI-enhanced upscaling" because they know most people don't know what any of it means — and confused people tend to buy the more expensive option "just in case."

Let's fix that. Here's what every spec actually means, whether you need it, and how to avoid overpaying for features that won't make a difference on your setup.

Resolution: 4K vs 1080p

What it means: 4K (also called Ultra HD or 2160p) has four times the pixels of 1080p (Full HD). More pixels = sharper, more detailed picture.

Do you need it? It depends on your TV.

  • If you have a 4K TV (most TVs sold since 2018 are 4K): Yes, get a 4K streaming device. You're leaving picture quality on the table otherwise.
  • If you have a 1080p TV: No. A 4K streaming device will just output 1080p anyway. Save your money and get the Fire TV Stick Lite.
  • If you're not sure: Check your TV's settings or Google the model number. But if you bought it in the last 5-6 years, it's almost certainly 4K.

HDR: Dolby Vision vs HDR10+ vs HDR10

What it means: HDR (High Dynamic Range) makes bright parts brighter and dark parts darker, with more colors in between. It's a bigger visual improvement than the jump from 1080p to 4K, honestly.

The formats:

  • HDR10: The baseline. Every 4K streaming device and most 4K TVs support it. You're covered.
  • Dolby Vision: The premium format. Dynamic metadata adjusts the picture scene by scene. Looks noticeably better than HDR10 on supported TVs. Netflix, Disney+, and Apple TV+ use it heavily.
  • HDR10+: Samsung's answer to Dolby Vision. Similar dynamic metadata, but less widely supported. Amazon Prime Video uses it.

Do you need it? If your TV supports Dolby Vision (check the specs), get a streaming device that supports it. The difference is visible and worth it. HDR10+ is a nice bonus but not a dealbreaker if your device doesn't have it.

Audio: Dolby Atmos

What it means: Dolby Atmos is surround sound that includes height channels — sound can come from above you, not just around you. It creates a more immersive audio experience for movies and shows that support it.

Do you need it? Only if you have a Dolby Atmos soundbar or surround sound system. If you're using your TV's built-in speakers, Atmos support on your streaming device is meaningless — the TV speakers can't reproduce it. Don't pay extra for Atmos if you don't have the audio equipment to use it.

WiFi: WiFi 6E vs WiFi 6 vs WiFi 5

What it means:

  • WiFi 5 (802.11ac): The previous standard. Perfectly fine for streaming, even 4K.
  • WiFi 6 (802.11ax): Faster, better at handling multiple devices, improved range.
  • WiFi 6E: Same as WiFi 6 but adds a 6 GHz band — less congestion, faster speeds, lower latency.

Do you need it? WiFi 5 is sufficient for 4K streaming. WiFi 6 is a nice upgrade if you have lots of devices on your network. WiFi 6E is only useful if you have a WiFi 6E router — and even then, the real-world difference for streaming is minimal. Don't choose a streaming device based on WiFi spec alone.

The one exception: if your streaming device is far from your router and you get buffering, a newer WiFi standard with better range can help.

Voice Control

The options:

  • Alexa (Fire TV): Best smart home integration, good content search, works with Echo devices
  • Google Assistant (Chromecast): Best natural language understanding, good for general questions
  • Siri (Apple TV): Best for Apple ecosystem, improving but still limited for TV
  • Roku Voice: Basic content search only, no smart home integration

Do you need it? Voice search is genuinely useful — saying "play Stranger Things" is faster than typing it with a remote. But voice-controlled smart home features only matter if you have smart home devices. Pick the assistant that matches your existing ecosystem.

Storage

Most streaming sticks have 8GB of storage. The Fire TV Cube has 16GB. Apple TV has 64GB or 128GB. NVIDIA Shield has 16GB.

Does it matter? For most people, 8GB is fine. Streaming apps are small, and you're streaming content, not storing it locally. You might need to uninstall an app occasionally if you have a lot installed, but it's rarely an issue. If you plan to install games or lots of niche apps, more storage helps.

Ethernet

Most streaming sticks are WiFi-only. The Fire TV Cube and some Apple TV models have built-in Ethernet. Others offer Ethernet adapters for $10-15.

Do you need it? If your WiFi is reliable, no. But if you experience buffering, especially with 4K content, a wired Ethernet connection is the most reliable fix. It's also better for anyone with a mesh WiFi system where the streaming device might connect to a distant node.

The Decision Flowchart

Still not sure? Here's the simple version:

  1. What's your budget?
    • Under $35 → Fire TV Stick Lite
    • $35-60 → Fire TV Stick 4K Max or Roku Streaming Stick 4K+
    • $100+ → Apple TV 4K or Fire TV Cube
  2. What ecosystem are you in?
    • Amazon/Alexa → Fire TV Stick
    • Apple/iPhone → Apple TV
    • Google/Android → Chromecast with Google TV
    • None/neutral → Roku or Fire TV Stick
  3. Is your TV 4K?
    • Yes → Get a 4K streaming device
    • No → Save money, get the Lite
